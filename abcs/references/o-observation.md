# O is for Observation

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Students learn by watching someone do the thing and copying it. They pick up the motion, and they pick up the attitude that comes with it. Show them a player who shrugs off a flub and keeps going, and they copy the shrug along with the fingering.

## Example and what it's good for

Play a barre chord in front of the class and let the first attempt buzz. Say what you hear, adjust your thumb, try again, and land it. Students watch the fix happen in real time. Use observation for anything hard to put in words: the shape of a breath, the angle of a picking hand, the calm of working through a rough passage.

- Demonstrate a tricky run at half speed, then at tempo, so they see the bridge between the two.
- Bring in a recording of a strong ensemble so students hear the blend you're asking for.

## Why it works

A model shows the whole action at once, including the parts you can't name. Verbal instruction breaks a skill into steps; watching delivers it as one integrated move. The affect matters as much as the mechanics. When students see you struggle and recover, they learn that struggle is part of the work, not a sign they lack talent.

## Problems it solves

- A skill resists description.
  - You can show a legato bow-arm gesture faster than you can explain it.
- Students read effort as failure.
  - Watching a skilled player rep a hard bar teaches that experts drill too.
- Beginners freeze at the gap between them and the goal.
  - Model the messy middle, not the finished product, so the path looks walkable.

## How to use it

- Demonstrate the process, including the errors and fixes. A flawless run hides how you got there.
- Narrate your attention. "I'm listening for the buzz on the low string right here."
- Use peer models. A tenth-grader who nailed the passage a minute ago is a closer target than you.
- Let students watch more than once. First pass for the whole, later passes for the detail.

## Risks

- A model that only ever succeeds teaches that good players don't struggle. Show the work, not the polish.
- Watching can substitute for doing. Observation primes the skill. Reps build it. Move students to their own instruments.
