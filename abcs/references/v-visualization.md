# V is for Visualization

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Turn something invisible or tangled into a picture the eye can scan. A chart, grid, or diagram holds structure in space so students stop juggling it in their heads and start reasoning about it.

## Example and what it's good for

Draw a chord grid on the board for your guitar class: six vertical lines for strings, horizontal lines for frets, dots where fingers press. A beginner who can't parse "second fret, third string" finds the dot in two seconds. The grid shows the shape of a G chord as one gestalt instead of four separate instructions to memorize.

For choir, sketch a formation diagram of the risers with each voice part shaded. Sopranos see they sit next to altos, and the blend problem you keep describing turns into a map they can point at.

Visualization earns its keep when the information is:

- Invisible in the moment, like the arc of a phrase or the plan for a rehearsal block.
- Too big to hold at once, like a semester timeline of concert dates and the pieces due before each.

## Why it works

Working memory is small and leaks. When a student pictures a chord fingering, tracks the beat, and reads pitches at the same time, something drops. A diagram parks part of that load on the page, so attention frees up for the hard part.

Spatial layout also carries meaning that words bury. Left-to-right shows time. Up and down shows pitch. Distance shows how far apart two things sit. A student reads those relationships from the shape before you say a word.

## Problems it solves

- A verbal instruction keeps slipping.
  - "Crescendo through bar 12, then back off" lives on a drawn dynamics arc that stays in front of them.
- A student can't see how the parts fit the whole.
  - A one-page map of a song's sections shows verse, chorus, and bridge as blocks, and the repeat that confused them becomes an arrow.

## How to use it

- Match the space to the structure. Time goes on a horizontal line, pitch on a vertical one, formation on a top-down floor plan.
- Build it with them, not before them. Draw the phrase arc as the section sings so the picture and the sound land together.
- Keep one diagram to one idea. A grid for the fingering, a separate arc for the dynamics.
- Hand the pen over. A student who diagrams their own practice week owns it more than one who copies yours.

## Risks

- A cluttered diagram hides the point it was meant to reveal. Strip it to the feature you're teaching.
- A picture can stand in for the sound. The chart serves the singing, so return to the ears before the lesson ends.
- Some students lean on the diagram forever. Fade it once the shape lives in their hands.
