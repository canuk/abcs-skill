# Z is for ZZZs (Sleep)

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Sleep does the second half of learning. During the day a student practices; during the night the brain consolidates it, filing facts into memory and smoothing motor skills. A skill you drilled at rehearsal is often better the next morning, after sleep, than it was when you left.

## Example and what it's good for

A guitar student grinds on a tricky chord change all afternoon and leaves frustrated, still fumbling it. They come back two days later and it flows. Nothing magic happened. Their brain rehearsed the motion during sleep and cleaned it up. The practice planted it; the sleep grew it.

This is why cramming the night before a concert backfires. A student who stays up drilling parts loses the sleep that would have locked in the week's work, and shows up foggy on a task that needs sharp attention and steady hands.

Sleep matters most for:

- Motor skills like chord changes, strumming patterns, and breath control, which consolidate hard overnight.
- Any material a student needs to still know next week, since sleep moves it into lasting memory.

## Why it works

While a teenager sleeps, the brain replays the day's patterns and strengthens the connections behind them. Facts get consolidated. Physical sequences get refined without conscious effort. Skip the sleep and that processing doesn't happen, so the day's gains stay fragile.

Sleep deprivation also wrecks the machinery of learning itself. A tired student can't hold attention, encodes new material worse, and reacts slower. High schoolers run short on sleep already, which means the room in front of you is often working at a deficit before you play a note.

## Problems it solves

- Practice seems stuck with no visible gain.
  - Spacing the same passage across several nights lets sleep consolidate between sessions, and the plateau breaks.
- Cram sessions don't hold.
  - Spreading prep over a week, with sleep between, beats one long night that the brain never gets to file.

## How to use it

- Space rehearsal of hard passages across days, so sleep works between reps.
- Introduce new music early in the week, not the night before, and let nights do the consolidating.
- Tell students the mechanism. A teenager who knows sleep locks in their part is likelier to protect it before a concert.
- Frame the pre-concert night as rest, not a final cram. They gain more from sleep than from one more run.

## Risks

- You can't make teenagers sleep. Aim your control at spacing the practice, which you set.
- "Sleep on it" can excuse skipping real practice. Consolidation needs something worth consolidating first.
- One good night won't fix chronic deprivation. The habit across weeks is what pays off.
