# C is for Contrasting Cases

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Put two things that seem the same side by side so the difference stands out. A distinction jumps out when you have a reference to compare it against. One example teaches the label. A well-chosen pair teaches the boundary.

## Example and what it's good for

Play two recordings of the same eight-bar phrase back to back. In the first, the section breathes together and stays centered on pitch. In the second, they sag flat on the sustained notes. Say nothing yet. Ask the students what's different. The ones who couldn't define "supported tone" will point straight at the drooping vowels once they have something to compare against. The contrast teaches. Your explanation afterward names what they already heard.

This move carries most of the perceptual work in a music room: ear training, tone quality, intonation, articulation, strumming feel, tempo. It also turns a vague vocabulary word into something concrete. Pair one example that has the quality with a near-identical one that lacks it, and "legato" stops being an abstraction.

## Why it works

Learning to perceive means learning what to attend to and what to ignore. When two examples differ on one dimension and hold everything else steady, that dimension jumps to the front of a student's attention. They also build the skill of catching the cue on their own later, which is what carries the learning past your classroom door.

## Problems it solves

- A student can't hear a distinction you keep pointing at.
  - "Sing it more legato" lands once they hear a legato line against a choppy version of the same phrase.
- A student treats two different things as the same.
  - Natural minor and harmonic minor blur together; a plain downstroke and a palm-muted downstroke sound identical to a beginner.
- A vocabulary word stays abstract.
  - "In tune" sharpens the moment they compare a clean unison against one that sits ten cents high.
- A student learns a rule but can't tell when to use it.
  - Contrasting cases teach the cue, not the rule alone, so a singer notices a note going flat without you flagging it.

## How to use it

- Build minimal pairs. Two examples, identical except for the one feature you're teaching. Change that feature and nothing else.
  - Same phrase, supported against unsupported.
  - Same progression, dragging against steady.
  - Same lick, clean against a buzzing fret hand.
- Play them back to back and ask the students to name the difference before you name it.
- For a bigger concept, vary two features at once. Loud-and-flat, loud-and-in-tune, soft-and-flat, soft-and-in-tune shows singers that volume and pitch move on separate tracks.
- Record the students. Today's take against last month's take makes a contrast they can't argue with.

## Risks

- Two cases that differ in many ways at once pull attention to the wrong feature. Keep the pair tight.
- A student can spot the difference and never ask why it matters. Follow the contrast with a question or a short explanation so noticing turns into understanding.
- A contrast too fine for their current ears frustrates more than it teaches. Widen the gap at first, then close it as they grow.
