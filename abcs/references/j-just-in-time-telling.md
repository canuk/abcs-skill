# J is for Just-in-Time Telling

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Let students struggle with a problem before you explain it. An explanation lands on a prepared mind. When a student has already tried and hit the wall, she has questions the explanation can answer. Tell her the answer first and the words slide past, because she has no slot to file them in.

## Example and what it's good for

You could open by teaching the rule that a clean chord change needs the fingers to move as one shape, not one finger at a time. Instead, hand out G and C and ask students to switch between them at tempo. They fumble. Fingers arrive late, the strum stops to wait, the change buzzes. Now they feel the exact problem. When you step in and show the fingers moving as a single block, the fix answers a question their hands were already asking. The struggle built the readiness.

## Why it works

A failed attempt does quiet work. It shows a student where the edges of her knowledge are and makes her notice which features of the problem matter. When your explanation arrives, it connects to those live questions instead of floating free. The telling still matters, and skipping it leaves students to reinvent everything, which is slow and error-prone. Timing is the whole point. The same words that bounce off before the struggle stick after it.

## Problems it solves

- Explanations students nod along to and then can't apply.
  - Let them attempt the task first, so your instruction answers a problem they can feel.
- Passive rehearsals where you talk and they wait.
  - Give them the hard passage cold, then teach into the specific mess they make.

## How to use it

- Assign a short challenge just past their current skill before you teach the technique behind it.
- Watch for the moment of productive stuck, then step in with the explanation.
- Keep the struggle brief. A minute or two of honest effort is enough to prime the telling.
- Tie your explanation to what you saw them do. "You felt that gap in the change. Here's why it happened."

## Risks

- Struggle with no resolution turns into discouragement. Always follow the attempt with the telling.
- A challenge pitched far beyond students produces flailing, not readiness. Set the task at the edge of what they can almost do.
