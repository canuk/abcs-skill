# F is for Feedback

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Feedback shows a student the gap between what they did and what they were aiming for, in a form they can act on. A student rarely hears their own flat note or feels their own rushed strum. Good feedback makes the gap audible and points at the next move to close it. Without it, practice locks in whatever the student is already doing.

## Example and what it's good for

A singer holds a phrase and drifts flat on the sustained vowel, and they have no idea. Telling them "listen better" gives them nothing to do. Instead, name it and hand them the fix. "The pitch sagged on the held note. Keep the air moving through it and lift the vowel." Now they know what happened and what to try next. Even sharper, play their recording back so they hear the sag themselves, then repeat the phrase right away.

Feedback is what turns a repetition into a correction instead of a rehearsal of the error.

## Why it works

A learner can't fix what they can't perceive, and performers are poor judges of their own output in the moment. Specific, timely feedback supplies the missing information while the attempt is still fresh enough to connect the note to the action. Naming one thing keeps the correction inside what the student can hold and act on in the next try.

## Problems it solves

- A student repeats the same mistake no matter how much they practice.
  - They can't detect it. External feedback surfaces the error so the reps start fixing it.
- A student hears "good job" or "needs work" and doesn't improve.
  - Vague verdicts give nothing to change. Point at the specific note and the specific next move.

## How to use it

- Be specific about what happened and what to do next. "Fret closer to the wire so the note stops buzzing" beats "cleaner."
- Give it close to the attempt, while the student still remembers the feel of it.
- Limit each round to one thing. A list of six corrections lands as noise.
- Use recordings so students learn to hear their own gaps and eventually feed back to themselves.

## Risks

- Too much at once overwhelms. Six corrections leave a student unsure which to fix, so they fix none.
- Feedback aimed at the person rather than the work stings without teaching. "You're careless" gives no action. Keep it on the note, the breath, the finger.
- Constant correction breeds dependence. Fade your input as students start catching their own errors.
