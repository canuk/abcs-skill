# Q is for Question Driven

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Hang a whole unit on one question worth answering. The question gives curiosity a target, and every skill you teach becomes a tool for closing in on the answer. Knowledge learned in pursuit of a question stays connected to the reason you needed it.

## Example and what it's good for

Open a four-week unit with one question on the board: "Why does our sound fall apart the second we split into parts?" Now sight-singing, interval drills, and listening across the room stop feeling like isolated exercises. Each one is a lead the students follow. By week four they can answer the question, and the answer holds their vocabulary for tuning, blend, and independence in a single frame.

For guitar, try "What makes a chord change sound clean instead of clunky?" Finger economy, anchor fingers, and strum timing all report back to that one question.

## Why it works

- People remember facts better when the facts answer something they wanted to know. The question is the hook the knowledge hangs on.
- A driving question sets a direction, so students can tell whether today's work moved them forward. That sense of progress feeds the next class.
- Skills taught in service of a shared goal end up linked to each other, not filed in separate mental drawers.

## Problems it solves

- Students who ask "why are we doing this?" have an answer built into the unit.
- Skills that felt like busywork gain a purpose once they serve the question.
  - A tuning drill is dull on its own. As evidence for why parts collapse, it earns attention.
- Isolated lessons that never add up get pulled into one arc.

## How to use it

- Write a question students can care about, not a topic. "Blend" is a topic. "Why can strangers in a pro choir blend in one rehearsal when we can't in six?" is a question.
- Keep it visible. Put it where students see it every class, and point back to it when you introduce a new drill.
- Let some lessons partly answer it and open a sharper follow-up. A living question grows as the unit goes.
- Close the loop. Spend a class where students state their answer and back it with what they practiced.

## Risks

- A question too broad ("What is music?") gives no traction. Too narrow ("What key is this in?") closes in a minute. Aim for one a unit's worth of work can answer.
- If you never return to the question, it becomes decoration and students stop believing the work connects to it.
- A question you assign but students don't own drives you, not them. Let them help shape it, or pick one you know their room already argues about.
