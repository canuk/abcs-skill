# S is for Self-Explanation

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Ask a student to explain the material to themselves, out loud or on paper. Why does this step work? What does this marking mean? The act of putting it into their own words builds a mental model and drags hidden gaps into the light.

## Example and what it's good for

A guitarist has the C-to-G change under their fingers but plays it stiff. Stop and ask: "Walk me through what each finger does between those chords." As they talk, they notice the ring finger travels the farthest and leaves last. They found their own bottleneck. Naming the motion did more than another ten reps would have.

In choir, hand a student a phrase with a crescendo into a fermata and ask why the composer marked it that way. Their answer, right or shaky, tells you both what they understand.

## Why it works

- Explaining forces a student to connect a new piece to what they already know. A fact you can justify is a fact you own.
- Gaps stay invisible while you nod along to a demo. They surface the moment you try to say why, and you hit the spot you can't fill.
- Students build a model they can run on new material, instead of a copy of the one example you showed.

## Problems it solves

- The student who mimics your fingering yet stalls the instant the song changes key. Explanation shifts them from copying to reasoning.
- Illusions of understanding. "I get it" collapses when they try to explain it and can't.
- Passive rehearsal where students wait to be told the next thing instead of predicting it.

## How to use it

- Ask "why" and "what does this mean," not "what comes next." Aim at the reason, not the label.
  - "Why do we breathe before that word and not the louder one two beats later?"
  - "What is this key signature telling your left hand before you play a note?"
- Prompt at the hard step, the place a student is about to guess. Explanation pays off most where reasoning is required.
- Give quiet think time. A student who explains too fast is often reciting, not reasoning.
- Let a wrong explanation stand long enough to reveal the flaw, then teach into it.

## Risks

- Self-explanation costs time. Use it at the sticking points, not on every measure, or rehearsal grinds to a halt.
- A confident student can explain fluently and still be wrong. Listen to the content, not the polish.
- Over-prompt and it turns into interrogation. Space the questions so students stay curious instead of bracing for the next one.
