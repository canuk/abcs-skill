# W is for Worked Examples

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Show the whole procedure, every step, before you ask a beginner to do it alone. A worked example is a finished demonstration they can study, not a problem they have to solve under pressure.

## Example and what it's good for

Teaching the F chord on guitar, walk through it out loud while they watch: barre the first fret with your index finger, drop the ring finger on the third fret of the fifth string, middle finger on the second fret third string, press from the tip. Play it slow. Play it again. Then let them try. They copy a model instead of guessing their way into a shape that already defeats most beginners.

For sight-singing, work a four-bar phrase on the board start to finish. Name the key, find do, count the rhythm aloud, then sing each interval as you point. The class sees the full path from cold notation to sung pitch before you hand them a new phrase.

Worked examples shine for:

- Brand-new procedures where trial and error wastes time and breeds bad habits.
- Multi-step skills where a student who stalls on step two never reaches step four.

## Why it works

A beginner solving a fresh problem spends most of their mental effort searching for what to do next. That search crowds out the learning. A worked example removes the search, so attention goes to the steps and how they connect.

Watching a clean model also builds an accurate picture of the goal. A student who has seen a correct F chord formed knows what their hand is aiming for, and can tell when their own is wrong.

## Problems it solves

- A student flails on a task with too many moving parts.
  - A full demonstration of a chord change, fingers lifting and landing together, gives them one clear thing to imitate.
- Practice cements errors.
  - Seeing the right breath and posture first means the reps that follow groove the correct motion.

## How to use it

- Narrate every step, including the ones you do without thinking, like anchoring a thumb behind the neck.
- Pair each example with one solo attempt, then fade your support as they get steady.
- Alternate worked and self-tried. Show one chord change, they do the next, you show the third.
- Leave blanks late. Once they have the pattern, hand them a half-worked example and let them finish it.

## Risks

- Watching feels like learning but isn't. Move them to their own attempt before the model goes stale.
- A worked example past its usefulness turns into a crutch. Fade it as skill grows.
- Skip your automatic steps and the beginner falls in the gap you jumped. Slow down and show the whole thing.
