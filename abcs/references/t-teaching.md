# T is for Teaching

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Put a student in the teacher's seat. To explain a skill to a peer, they have to organize it, sequence it, and find words for it, and that effort deepens their own grasp more than one more round of practice would.

## Example and what it's good for

Pair a student who nailed the alto entrance with one still fishing for the note. The stronger singer has to figure out how she finds the pitch: off the tenor below, off the piano, off the phrase before. She never spelled that out until she had to teach it. Now she owns it. The learner gets help, and the teacher gets the bigger gain.

On guitar, run a jigsaw: one group masters the verse strum, another the chorus, and each teaches the other. Nobody hides, because everybody is on the hook to deliver.

## Why it works

- Preparing to teach forces you to structure loose knowledge into an order someone else can follow. Building that order is where understanding hardens.
- A learner's questions expose the spots the teacher glossed over, so the teacher patches gaps they didn't know they had.
- Explaining out loud is retrieval under pressure. Pulling the skill up to hand it over strengthens the student's own hold on it.

## Problems it solves

- Fast students who coast after they've got it. Teaching turns their spare capacity into deeper learning.
- Uneven sections where a few carry the notes. Peer teaching spreads the knowledge and the responsibility.
- Your own bandwidth. You can't sit with thirty students at once, but they can sit with each other.

## How to use it

- Give the student-teacher a narrow, clear target. "Teach him the alto entrance," not "help him with the song."
- Have them prepare briefly first. A minute to plan how they'll explain it lifts the quality of the teaching.
- Rotate roles so today's teacher is tomorrow's learner. No one gets typecast as the weak one.
- Listen in. When a student teaches something wrong, you've found a misconception to fix at the source.

## Risks

- A student who teaches a bad habit spreads it. Spot-check what's being passed along.
- Pairing can wound if the same students always teach and the same ones always receive. Mix it up.
- Teaching eats rehearsal time. Use it where the depth is worth the minutes, not for drills a quick correction would settle.
