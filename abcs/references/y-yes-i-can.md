# Y is for Yes I Can

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Self-efficacy is a student's belief that they can succeed at this specific task. It's not general confidence. It's "I can play this chord change" or "I can hit that entrance." That belief drives whether they try, how long they persist, and how they read a setback.

## Example and what it's good for

A guitar student stalls on switching between C and G. Instead of praising talent, you name the strategy: "You kept your ring finger anchored that time, and the change got smoother." Now they know what worked and that effort moved it. Next practice, they attribute progress to something they control, and they keep going.

Bring in a near-peer model. A sophomore who struggled with barre chords last year demonstrates them today. The beginner watching thinks, that person is like me and they got it, so I can too. A polished senior wouldn't send the same signal.

Self-efficacy is your tool for:

- Students convinced they're "not musical" who quit before a skill has a chance to form.
- The gap between a hard task and a beginner's willingness to attempt it.

## Why it works

A student who believes they can succeed invests effort and treats a mistake as information. One who doubts they can reads the same mistake as proof they should stop. The belief steers the interpretation.

Belief comes from real sources: small wins they can feel, watching someone like them succeed, and specific feedback that ties results to effort and method. Praise for a fixed trait ("you're a natural") does the opposite, because when the task gets hard the student decides the trait ran out.

## Problems it solves

- A student gives up the moment a passage gets hard.
  - Breaking it into a reachable first step lets them feel a win, and the win rebuilds the belief that fuels the next attempt.
- "I can't sing" becomes a self-fulfilling ceiling.
  - Structured early successes and near-peer models chip at the belief before it hardens.

## How to use it

- Praise effort and strategy, never fixed talent. Name the specific thing they did that worked.
- Engineer early wins. Start with a task inside reach, so the first rep succeeds.
- Use near-peer models. A student one step ahead proves the skill is gettable.
- Point backward at progress. "Last month this phrase stopped you, and today you sang it clean."

## Risks

- Hollow praise for easy work reads as fake, and students stop trusting your feedback. Tie it to real effort.
- Wins that are too easy build brittle confidence that cracks on the first real challenge. Keep the task honest.
- Over-scaffolding steals the sense of authorship. Let them own the success rather than receive it.
