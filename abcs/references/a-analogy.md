# A is for Analogy

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

An analogy maps the structure of something a student already understands onto something new. The surface details differ. The underlying relationship holds. When a student sees that the same principle runs through two different situations, they can carry what they know into the unfamiliar one.

## Example and what it's good for

A guitarist keeps mashing down on the fretboard and wonders why their hand cramps by the second song. Compare it to how they hold a full cup of coffee without crushing it. Enough grip to keep it steady, no more. Now they have a target for fret pressure that isn't a number, it's a feel they already own.

You can run the same move across choir and guitar. Breath support under a long phrase works like the steady pressure that keeps a bike upright once it's rolling. Coasting drops the tone. In both cases you're teaching students to find the shared principle instead of memorizing two separate rules.

## Why it works

Students build new understanding out of what they already have. A good analogy hands them a ready-made structure so they don't start from nothing. When they line up the parts of the familiar case against the parts of the new one, the relationship, not the trivia, is what transfers.

## Problems it solves

- A concept feels abstract and won't stick.
  - "Resonance" means little until you compare a singer's chest and head spaces to the hollow body of an acoustic guitar amplifying a plucked string.
- A student can do a skill in one context but not a new one.
  - Naming the shared principle helps them see that a ii-V-I in G is the same move they already learned in C.

## How to use it

- Start from what the student already knows well, then map it piece by piece onto the new material.
- Ask students to generate their own analogies. Their comparison shows you what they think the principle is.
- Name where the analogy breaks so they don't overextend it.

## Risks

- Every analogy fits in some ways and fails in others. If you skip the mismatch, students carry the wrong part over. Coffee-cup grip teaches pressure, not the angle of the thumb.
- A flashy comparison can entertain without teaching. Check that the shared structure, not the surface story, is what students take away.
