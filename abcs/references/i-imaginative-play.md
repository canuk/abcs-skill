# I is for Imaginative Play

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Ask students to pretend, and their thinking climbs. When a student takes on a character or holds an image in mind, she runs a symbol in her head and acts as if it were real. That act stretches two muscles at once, the symbolic imagination and the self-control it takes to stay in a role.

## Example and what it's good for

Your choir sings the notes of a lament with the emotional weight of a grocery list. Stop and set a scene. Tell them the phrase is a letter to someone they will not see again, and the last line is the part they almost couldn't write. Sing it again as that person. The pitches don't change. The tone, the breath, the shape of the vowels all shift because the students are inhabiting a situation. Imaginative play suits expression, dynamics, stage presence, and the nerve it takes to perform.

## Why it works

Pretending is symbolic work. A student who imagines herself as a soloist in a grand hall is manipulating a mental stand-in for a real experience, and that rehearsal in imagination shapes real behavior. Holding a role also demands regulation. To stay in character through a hard passage, a student overrides the urge to shrink or laugh it off. That is the same control she needs to hold her nerve on stage, practiced in a low-stakes frame.

## Problems it solves

- Technically clean singing that lands flat and lifeless.
  - Give the phrase a character and a stake, and the expression follows without a lecture on feeling.
- Stage fright and self-consciousness.
  - Have students rehearse "as if" they were the confident performer, so poise becomes a role they can step into.

## How to use it

- Attach an image or a character to a passage that needs color. Let the imagined situation carry the interpretation.
- Run a mock performance where students play the part of a poised musician, curtain to bow.
- Use metaphor as a costume for a technique. "Sing like you're telling a secret" moves dynamics more than "sing quieter."
- Keep the frame concrete. A specific person, place, and stake beats a vague instruction to "feel it."

## Risks

- Some older students read pretend as childish and resist. Frame it as an actor's tool, not a game.
- Play can drift into performance for its own sake and lose the musical target. Tie every scene back to the sound you want.
