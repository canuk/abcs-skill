# M is for Making

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Building a real thing sets its own goals. A student who commits to recording a song hears every flat note and dropped beat, because the artifact will outlast the moment. The thing you make talks back.

## Example and what it's good for

Have each guitar student produce one polished cover to hand in as an audio file. Suddenly the strum they'd wave off in class matters, because it's going on the recording. They loop the hard bar, re-track it, listen again. Use making when you want students to care about precision without you having to demand it.

- A choir writing a short arrangement for the winter concert argues over voicing because the audience is real.
- A student building a chord-chart binder for their own songs practices reading because they'll use it.

## Why it works

An artifact creates a feedback loop the student runs on their own. They set a goal, produce a draft, compare it to what they wanted, and revise. Ownership grows because the work is theirs and headed somewhere. That interest keeps them practicing the dull parts, since the dull parts stand between them and a thing they want to exist.

## Problems it solves

- Practice feels pointless with no destination.
  - A recording, a performance, or a written arrangement gives the reps a target.
- Students accept "good enough" in rehearsal.
  - A permanent artifact raises the bar without you nagging.
- Motivation runs on your energy instead of theirs.
  - When the work is theirs to finish, the drive comes from them.

## How to use it

- Make the artifact real and public. A recording others will hear beats a worksheet.
- Give room for choice. Let students pick the song or write the arrangement.
- Build in revision. The first take is a draft, not the deliverable.
- Show the loop. Play take one against take four so they hear their own progress.

## Risks

- The product can eat the learning. A slick recording built on copied parts teaches editing software, not musicianship. Keep the target skill in the frame.
- Big projects can overwhelm beginners. Scope the make so a novice can finish one and feel it.
