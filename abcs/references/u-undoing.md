# U is for Undoing

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

A wrong idea or a worn-in bad habit shrugs off simple correction. Telling a student "not like that" leaves the faulty model in place, ready to snap back. You have to surface the belief underneath the error and help the student build a better one that competes and wins.

## Example and what it's good for

A guitarist mutes strings every time they reach a G chord. You keep saying "arch your fingers," and nothing sticks. Dig underneath, and you find the belief: they think pressing harder makes cleaner notes, so they flatten their whole hand into the fretboard. The real fix targets that idea. Show them a light, arched finger ringing clear next to a mashed one buzzing, and let them feel that less pressure with better placement beats brute force. Now they have a rival model that explains what they hear.

In choir, a singer who scoops up to every note believes that's how you find the pitch safely. Correcting the scoop alone fails until you replace the belief with a way to hear the note before singing it.

## Why it works

- A habit or a wrong idea isn't empty space you can write over. It's a real structure the student trusts, and it stays active until something better outcompetes it.
- Once you name the belief driving the error, the student can examine it instead of defending it on reflex.
- A competing model backed by evidence the student can feel or hear gives the brain a reason to reach for the new pattern under pressure.

## Problems it solves

- Errors that return the moment you stop watching. The old model was never replaced, only suppressed.
- Corrections that don't transfer. A student fixes one G chord but mangles the next new chord the same way, because the underlying belief is intact.
- Frustration on both sides when repeating the right instruction louder changes nothing.

## How to use it

- Find the misconception first. Ask what the student thinks is happening before you correct the motion.
  - "What do you think makes a note come out clean?"
  - "When you scoop, what are you listening for?"
- Make the flaw visible. Contrast the wrong version with the right one so the student feels the gap themselves.
- Build the replacement and rehearse it under easy conditions before real pressure, so the new pattern has a chance to take hold.
- Expect relapse. The old habit resurfaces when a student is tired or nervous. Name that in advance so they don't read it as failure.

## Risks

- Skip the underlying belief and you're trimming symptoms. The habit regrows from a root you never touched.
- Undoing takes longer than teaching fresh, and it can feel like backsliding. Warn students so they don't quit mid-repair.
- Prying at a comfortable habit can rattle a student's confidence. Keep the new model concrete and reachable so they have somewhere solid to stand.
