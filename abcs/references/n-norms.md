# N is for Norms

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Every room runs on unwritten rules about what's normal. Whether a wrong note is a disaster or a data point, whether asking to hear a part again is smart or weak. Students read those rules fast and play to them. You can set them on purpose or let them set themselves.

## Example and what it's good for

Decide that in your room a cracked high note earns a nod, not a wince, and hold to it every time one happens. Within a few weeks students take vocal risks they'd have hidden before, because the norm says trying is safe. Use norms when you want behavior that no single instruction can produce: risk-taking, honest self-report, quick recovery from mistakes.

- If you always stop and fix intonation the second it drifts, students learn that tuning is worth attention.
- If you let one student mock another's playing once, you've set a norm that silences the room.

## Why it works

Students infer the rules from what you reward, ignore, and repeat, more than from what you announce. A poster saying "mistakes are welcome" means nothing if you sigh at every fumble. Norms work because they run in the background, shaping a hundred small choices you'd never have time to direct one by one.

## Problems it solves

- Students hide struggle, so you can't see what to teach.
  - A norm that treats errors as information brings the struggle into the open.
- Sectionals dissolve into chatter the moment you step away.
  - A norm of "the section leader runs it" holds the structure without you present.
- Feedback stings and shuts students down.
  - A shared norm about how you give and take notes makes critique usable.

## How to use it

- Name the norm you want, then model it under pressure. The first mistake after you announce it is the test.
- Reward the behavior in the moment. Catch a student asking for a replay and treat it as the smart move.
- Watch what you tolerate. The eye-roll you let slide becomes the rule.
- Hand norms to students. Let section leaders enforce the ones they own.

## Risks

- Norms form whether you tend them or not. Ignore them and the loudest students write them for you.
- A stated norm you break yourself dies fast. Students trust what you do over what you say.
