# G is for Generation

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Make students pull an answer out of memory instead of handing it back to them. The act of reaching for something you half-remember does more for retention than reading it a second time. When a student re-sings a passage from the page, the notes stay on the page. When she sings it with the page closed, the notes move into her.

## Example and what it's good for

You taught the G-C-D chord loop on Monday. On Wednesday, before any warmup, you say the three chord names and ask students to play the loop from memory, no chart. They stumble, hunt for the finger shapes, land some and miss others. That hunt is the work. Compare it to letting them stare at the chord diagram while they play, where their fingers follow the picture and their memory sits idle. Generation suits chord shapes, sight-singing intervals, lyrics, and scale patterns, anything you want retrievable under performance pressure.

## Why it works

Each time a student reconstructs a memory, she rebuilds the path to it, and the path gets easier to travel next time. Effortful recall marks a memory as worth keeping. Passive review feels smooth and familiar, and that smoothness fools students into thinking they know something they can only recognize, not produce. Spacing the attempts across days forces the memory to be rebuilt from a colder start, which strengthens it more than three attempts crammed into one afternoon.

## Problems it solves

- Students who nail a piece in rehearsal and freeze at the concert.
  - They practiced recognition, not retrieval. Close the book sooner.
- The illusion of mastery from re-listening to a recording.
  - Ask them to hum the alto line before you play it, then check.

## How to use it

- Start rehearsal with a cold recall of yesterday's material before you re-teach anything.
- Cover the chord chart once fingers find the first shape. Let them reconstruct the rest.
- Space it. Revisit Monday's material Wednesday and again the next week, not all at once.
- Give partial cues when they're stuck. A single starting pitch or the first chord name is enough scaffolding.

## Risks

- Recall that fails with no support builds frustration, not memory. Offer a hint before a student quits.
- Generation is slower than telling, and a packed rehearsal tempts you to skip it. The slowness is where the durable learning happens.
