# E is for Elaboration

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Elaboration ties new information to things a student already knows. A bare fact sits alone in memory with no path leading back to it. When a student links it to a story, an image, or something familiar, they build extra routes for retrieval. The richer the web of connections, the easier the fact is to find later.

## Example and what it's good for

Students need the order of sharps as they add key signatures. A raw list of letters slides right out of memory. Hand them a sentence where each word starts with the next sharp, something the class makes up together and finds a little ridiculous, and the order sticks because it now rides on a phrase they can hear.

The same works for guitar. A beginner learning the open-string names remembers them once they hang the letters on a phrase they invented rather than reciting six letters cold. The point isn't the trick itself. It's that they wove the new facts into knowledge they already carry.

## Why it works

Memory retrieves by association. A fact with one thin connection has one fragile path back, and if that path fails the fact is gone. Elaboration adds paths. A meaningful link, a vivid image, a small story each give the mind another way in. Making the connection also forces the student to process the material, and that processing is itself what lays down the trace.

## Problems it solves

- Facts memorized for a quiz evaporate by the next week.
  - Connecting each fact to something the student cares about or already knows gives it staying power.
- A student can recite a rule but can't call it up when playing.
  - A vivid association gives them a fast handle to grab mid-performance.

## How to use it

- Ask students to explain a new idea in their own words and link it to something from their life.
- Let students build their own mnemonics. A connection they made beats one they were handed.
- Prompt with "what does this remind you of" so they hunt for the tie to prior knowledge.

## Risks

- A clever mnemonic can crowd out the real understanding if it becomes the only thing a student holds. Use it as a handle, not a substitute for knowing why.
- Connections a student didn't make themselves often don't stick. Your association makes sense to you and stays foreign to them.
