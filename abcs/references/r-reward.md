# R is for Reward

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

A reward that follows a behavior makes the behavior more likely to return. That is real, and it is a blunt tool. Handle it with care, because paying students for things they already enjoy can teach them to stop enjoying those things.

## Example and what it's good for

Your first-period choir drifts in late and takes ten minutes to settle. You start a routine: whenever the full section is in seats and warmed up by the bell, you drop a marble in a jar, and a full jar buys a lighter Friday rehearsal. Attendance and setup tighten within two weeks. Rewards work well here because you are shaping a habit, a logistics problem, not the music itself.

## Why it works

Behavior that gets a payoff repeats. For clear, mechanical routines, a small consistent reward gives students a reason to start before the intrinsic reason has time to grow. Once the habit runs on its own, the reward has done its job.

## Problems it solves

- Setup, attendance, packing up gear, tuning before the bell: dull tasks with no built-in joy, where a nudge helps.
- Getting a reluctant beginner over the first ugly week of guitar, when fingertips hurt and nothing sounds good yet.

## The overjustification risk (read this twice)

This is the danger that makes reward the trickiest letter. When you pay a student to do something they already love, they can start reading their own effort as work-for-pay. Remove the pay, and the love is weaker than before you started.

- Hand out candy every time a student sings a solo they used to volunteer for, and you may find they stop volunteering once the candy ends.
- Grade the joy out of free improvisation on guitar, and students play for the rubric, not the sound.
- A student who practiced for the feeling of a clean barre chord now practices for points, and the feeling fades into the background.

The rule: never attach rewards to the parts of music you want students to fall in love with. Reward the boring scaffolding, protect the music itself.

## How to use it

- Reward behavior and habit, not artistry or engagement that already exists.
- Keep it small, keep it temporary, and plan the exit. Name the day the marble jar retires.
- Prefer rewards tied to the group and the activity, like an easier rehearsal, over tokens students pocket.
- Watch for the love. If students light up doing something, that is your signal to leave it unrewarded.

## Risks

- Overjustification, above all. Rewarding intrinsic motivation can erode it, and the damage outlasts the reward.
- Students escalate. What thrilled them in September is expected by October, and you climb a ladder you can't sustain.
- Rewards aimed at a group can punish the kid who tried hardest when the group still fails. Watch who carries the blame.
