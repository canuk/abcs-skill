# B is for Belonging

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

When a student believes they are a real member of the group and that the group wants them there, they invest more and quit less. A student who is unsure whether they belong reads every stumble as evidence they don't. That doubt drains attention away from the music and toward self-monitoring.

## Example and what it's good for

A new guitar student botches a chord change in front of the class and goes quiet for the rest of the period. What they do next depends on the story they tell themselves. "People flub that change for weeks, then it clicks" keeps them playing. "I'm the only one who can't do this" starts them counting reasons to drop the elective.

You shape that story. Tell the class that cracked notes and buzzing frets are what the room sounds like while it learns, and that everyone in the section went through the same rough patch. You give a shaky student a reason to stay.

## Why it works

A student who wonders whether they fit spends working memory on that question instead of on the task. Reassurance that struggle is normal and shared frees up that attention. Belonging also changes how a student reads setbacks. Once they trust they're a member, a bad rehearsal becomes a Tuesday, not a verdict.

## Problems it solves

- A capable student underperforms and you can't find a skill gap.
  - Often the drag is doubt about fitting in, not the notes. Address the doubt and the playing loosens.
- A student from a group that rarely sees itself on the roster hangs back.
  - Naming that many strong musicians felt like outsiders early on can steady them.

## How to use it

- Frame difficulty as a stage everyone passes through, not a sign of who has talent.
- Learn names fast and use them. Being known is the first evidence of belonging.
- Give every student a real part in the ensemble so their presence matters to the sound.

## Risks

- Empty praise backfires. A student knows when they sang flat, and cheerful denial tells them you can't be trusted about the good news either.
- Belonging built on lowered standards collapses the first time the group is judged. Pair the welcome with a genuine expectation that they can meet the bar.
