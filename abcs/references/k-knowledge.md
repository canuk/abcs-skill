# K is for Knowledge

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

Every letter in this toolkit points at one target: the knowledge your students carry out of your room. Knowledge is not one thing. A student who can sing a memorized alto line in tune, on time, every rehearsal, holds one kind of knowledge. A student who can hear that same line clash with a new soprano part and adjust the vowel and the dynamic to fix it holds another kind. You want both living in the same singer. This essay is about the two kinds, why the order you build them in matters, and how you decide, lesson by lesson, which one you are growing today.

## Two kinds of expertise

Picture knowledge on two axes. One axis measures efficiency: how fast, how accurate, how automatic a student is at a routine she already knows. The other measures innovation: how well she handles music, problems, and situations she has never met. A guitar student who plays a clean G-to-C change without looking, ten times in a row, at tempo, sits far out on the efficiency axis. A guitar student who lands in a jam, hears the group move to a key she never practiced, and finds workable chord shapes on the spot sits far out on the innovation axis.

Most people assume expertise means only the first axis. Faster, cleaner, more automatic. That describes a routine expert, and routine expertise is real and worth building. Your section leaders who nail every entrance, your guitarist who can sight-read a lead sheet in first position without stumbling, they have earned something hard. The risk is that routine expertise can turn brittle. A singer who learned her part by rote, matched to one recording, can fall apart when you reseat the ensemble or change the tempo. She is fast at the exact thing she drilled and lost the moment the thing changes.

The learner you want combines both axes. Researchers call this adaptive expertise: enough efficiency to execute without burning all her attention, plus enough innovation to bend that skill toward music she has not seen. The adaptive guitarist knows her open chords cold, and she can also reason about what a barre chord is doing well enough to invent a fingering when her usual one sits buried under a capo.

## Two ways to fail

Two failure modes sit at the far corners of that picture, and you have met students in both.

The frustrated novice lives on struggle with no efficiency to show for it. She explores, she experiments, she never consolidates. In a choir, this is the student you let improvise a harmony every day who still cannot match pitch to a given note, because no one ever stopped the exploring long enough to drill the matching. Struggle without payoff drains her, and she starts to believe she is unmusical.

The over-drilled routine expert sits at the opposite corner. He is fast and accurate inside a narrow box and helpless one inch outside it. Your guitarist who memorized one song from a video, note for note, and cannot play any of those same chords inside a different song, is a routine expert with nowhere to send his skill. He looks strong until the music moves.

Adaptive expertise sits between and above these corners. Your task across a semester is to keep pulling students off both corners and toward the middle-high ground where efficiency and innovation reinforce each other.

## The sequencing most teachers reverse

Here is the insight that changes lesson planning. Efficiency and innovation are two goals you build in a particular order, and the intuitive order runs backwards.

The tempting move is to tell first and let students apply after. You explain the rule, demonstrate the clean technique, then have students drill it. Efficient, tidy, and it produces shallow knowledge that does not transfer. Students who receive the answer before they feel the question absorb the procedure without the reason. They can repeat it and cannot rebuild it.

Turn it around. Give students an innovation experience first. Let them grapple with a problem that the eventual technique solves, before you name the technique. When you hand a beginning guitar class a song with an F chord and ask them to play it with only the open chords they know, they struggle, and inside that struggle they feel exactly why a barre exists. The finger runs out of frets. The reach fails. They generate their own clumsy workarounds. Then you teach the barre chord, and it lands on prepared ground, because they already own the problem it answers. This is Just-in-Time Telling, letter J of this toolkit. The struggle first, the telling second.

The same order works in choir. Before you teach the rule that a leading tone wants to resolve up a half step, play a phrase that stops on the leading tone and let it hang there, unresolved, until the sound bothers them. Ask them to sing where it "wants" to go. They will find the tonic by ear, most of them, without knowing the word. Now you name what they did. The label attaches to a sensation they already had, and it holds in a way a definition on a slide never would.

Innovation first does more than motivate. When students invent, fail, and notice distinctions before you supply the expert move, they build knowledge with more hooks in it. They learned what the technique is for, which is the thing that lets them reach for it in new music later. That reach is transfer, and transfer is the whole point.

## Balance, and why neither extreme works

None of this makes exploration sacred. Pure discovery, students left to invent everything with no telling, wastes the hours you do not have and manufactures frustrated novices. A choir that only improvises never learns to read. A guitar class that only jams never builds the clean chord changes that make jamming sound good. Struggle has to pay off in consolidation, or it stays mere struggle.

Pure drill fails at the other end. A rehearsal that only runs the same eight measures produces a section that can sing those measures and cannot sight-read the next piece. Efficiency with no innovation gives you performers who depend on you for every new situation.

The skill is choosing where a given lesson sits and moving across a unit on purpose. Early in a new piece, lean toward innovation. Let the choir hear the harmony, hunt for their notes, wrestle with the hard interval. Later in the same unit, lean hard into efficiency. Drill the entrances, polish the vowels, run the transitions until they are automatic, so the singers can spend their attention on expression during the concert instead of on remembering which note comes next. A unit that swings from exploration toward drill, by design, uses both axes in the order that builds adaptive expertise.

## How this shows up in a music room

Sight-singing is efficiency training. You want the solfege reflex automatic, do-re-mi mapped to sound without conscious thought, so drill it and drill it. But seed innovation by handing them a melody with a tricky leap and asking them to predict the hard spot before they sing it. They diagnose, then they drill.

Learning repertoire runs the full swing. Exploration when the piece is new, drill as the concert nears. Name which phase you are in so students know whether today rewards experimenting or precision.

Improvisation is innovation training. A guitarist trading blues licks is inventing inside a structure. It works only if the underlying efficiency is there, the pentatonic shape under the fingers, so the drill and the improv feed each other rather than compete.

Technique and drills are efficiency, plainly. Chord changes, scales, breath support, vowel shapes. Keep them, and keep asking why the technique exists, so the efficiency does not calcify into a trick that works on one song and nowhere else.

Preparing for performance is where efficiency earns its keep. The automatic parts free the mind for the parts that cannot be automatic, the listening, the blend, the response to a conductor's live cue. A singer with no automatic skill has no attention left for artistry.

Mixed-level ensembles let you assign the two kinds by readiness. Your advanced guitarist gets an innovation task, arrange this melody for the group. Your beginner gets an efficiency task, own these two chords by Friday. Same song, different corner of the knowledge picture, both moving.

## How to use this lens when planning

Before you plan a lesson, ask which kind of knowledge it grows. Efficiency, innovation, or a deliberate swing between them. Answer it in one sentence and let that answer shape the minutes.

When you introduce something new, resist telling first. Build a short struggle that makes students want the answer, then tell. The barre chord after the failed reach. The resolution after the unresolved phrase. Guard the telling so the struggle stays productive and does not curdle into the frustrated-novice trap. Watch for the moment they have wrestled enough, and step in.

When you assess, decide what you are measuring. A mastery test rewards efficiency: play the change at tempo, sing the line in tune. A transfer test rewards innovation: here is a chord you have not drilled, work out a fingering; here is a new harmony, find your note. Grade the two on purpose, because a semester of only mastery tests quietly tells students that music is memorizing, and a semester of only transfer tasks leaves them without the automatic skill that makes music feel good to make.

Across a unit, sketch the arc from exploration toward drill and check that you traveled it. If every rehearsal was drill, you built routine experts who will stall on the next piece. If every rehearsal was exploration, you built enthusiasm on a weak foundation. The knowledge you are after lives in the swing between the two, and you are the one who steers it there.
