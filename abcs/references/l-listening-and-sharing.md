# L is for Listening and Sharing

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Two students learn more from each other when the task needs both of them. Give them a job one person can finish alone and they split it. Give them a job that stalls without real listening and they build on what the other one offers.

## Example and what it's good for

Pair a guitarist who has clean chord shapes with one who keeps steady time but fumbles transitions. Ask them to arrange a verse together: one plays, one listens and calls the spot where the change lags, then they swap. Neither can complete the arrangement without hearing the other. Use this when you want students to notice detail you'd otherwise have to point out yourself.

- In choir, hand a small group one phrase and ask them to agree on where the breath goes. They have to sing it, disagree, and test both options.
- The task carries the lesson. You circulate and nudge.

## Why it works

Attention narrows when you perform and widens when you listen. A student playing a barre chord is tracking their own left hand. The partner watching that same hand has spare attention for the timing, the buzz, the release. Sharing pools two partial views into one fuller picture, and explaining a fix out loud forces the explainer to make it concrete.

## Problems it solves

- One student dominates and the other coasts.
  - Assign roles that rotate, so the quiet partner holds a job only they can do.
- Feedback from you doesn't stick because students hear it as correction.
  - A peer naming the same wobble lands as information, not judgment.
- A task feels like busywork.
  - Build it so the answer depends on both voices.

## How to use it

- Design group-worthy tasks. If one strong student can solve it solo, redesign it.
- Give a shared object of attention: one score, one recording, one phrase to fix.
- Name the listening move you want. "Tell your partner one thing that worked before one thing to change."
- Keep pairs small. Two students share more airtime than five.

## Risks

- Grouping students does not make them collaborate. Without a task that needs everyone, you get parallel play or a passenger.
- Mixed pairs can drift into tutor-and-tutee. Rotate roles so both sing, play, and coach.
