# X is for Excitement

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Emotional arousal sharpens attention and stamps experiences into memory. A moderate dose helps a student learn and perform. Too much tips into anxiety and choking. The relationship is an inverted U: performance climbs with excitement, peaks, then falls as nerves take over.

## Example and what it's good for

The week before a concert, a little edge in the room helps. Students focus, run the hard passage again, and remember the fix because the moment mattered to them. That same student, alone at a solo audition with their heart pounding, forgets the entrance they nailed a hundred times. The arousal that helped in rehearsal now floods the working memory they need for the notes.

Excitement is your lever for:

- Lifting a flat rehearsal, where a countdown to performance or a run-through for guests wakes the room up.
- Marking the moments you want remembered, since a charged first performance of a piece sticks harder than a routine repeat.

## Why it works

Arousal narrows attention onto what feels important and tells the brain this is worth keeping. A stirred-up student notices more and retains more of the right thing.

Past the peak, the same system turns on them. High anxiety fills working memory with worry about the outcome, and there's no room left for the pitch, the breath, the next chord. A skill that ran on autopilot now gets second-guessed step by step, and it falls apart.

## Problems it solves

- A rehearsal has gone lifeless and nothing sticks.
  - Raising the stakes with a timed run or a small audience brings focus back and makes the session memorable.
- A dry drill won't lodge in memory.
  - Tying it to something students care about, a piece they chose, gives it the emotional weight that helps it last.

## How to use it

- Read the room and aim for the middle of the curve. Add energy to a sluggish group, lower it for a jittery one.
- Rehearse under performance conditions early, so the arousal of the real thing feels familiar, not foreign.
- Teach a reset: slow breath, feet grounded, eyes on you. A calming routine pulls a student back down the curve.
- Frame nerves as fuel, not failure. A student who expects some buzz panics less when it arrives.

## Risks

- Push the stakes too high and you manufacture the anxiety that causes choking. Watch for the tipping point.
- A student prone to performance anxiety needs less pressure, not more. Read individuals, not the group as a whole.
- Over time, constant high stakes burn students out and sour them on performing. Save the big charge for when it counts.
