# P is for Participation

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

A beginner joins the real activity by owning a small piece of it. You scaffold the hard parts so a novice can hold one genuine job in a real performance. As they grow, you pull the support away and hand them more.

## Example and what it's good for

A first-week guitarist can't play the full progression, so you give them one chord to hit on the downbeat while the group plays the rest. They're in the song, contributing a real part, hearing how it fits. Over weeks you add the second chord, then the change between them, then the strum. Use participation when the full activity would drown a beginner but you want them inside it, not watching from the bench.

- A new singer takes the melody on one phrase and rests on the harder runs, then claims more each rehearsal.
- A student learning to read joins the sight-singing by tracking rhythm only, then adds pitch later.

## Why it works

Doing a real fraction of the task beats drilling an isolated fragment. The beginner feels how their part sits in the whole, and the whole is what gives the part meaning. Scaffolding lets them succeed at something authentic today, and that success buys the patience for the reps tomorrow. Fading the support moves the work from you to them without a jarring cliff.

## Problems it solves

- Beginners stuck on drills never touch the actual music.
  - A scaffolded real part puts them in the ensemble on day one.
- The gap between exercise and performance feels huge.
  - Participation shrinks it. The exercise is the performance, sized down.
- Students lose motivation practicing pieces in isolation.
  - Contributing to a real song keeps the goal in view.

## How to use it

- Carve out an authentic small part, not a fake one. One real chord beats a silent air-guitar.
- Scaffold the rest. Cover the parts they can't reach yet with the group or a backing track.
- Fade on a plan. Add one demand at a time and name what's coming next.
- Keep the whole audible. Let them hear the finished thing their part lives inside.

## Risks

- Scaffolds that never fade become permanent crutches. Schedule their removal from the start.
- A "part" too small to matter reads as busywork. The contribution has to be real enough that the music would miss it.
