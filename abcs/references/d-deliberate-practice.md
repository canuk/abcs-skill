# D is for Deliberate Practice

*Part of the ABCs teaching toolkit, adapted for a high school choir & guitar classroom. Framework inspired by Schwartz, Tsang & Blair, "The ABCs of How We Learn" (2016); explanations and examples are original.*

## Core learning mechanic

Deliberate practice aims effort at one specific weakness sitting a notch past what a student can already do. It runs on tight loops. Attempt, feedback, adjust, repeat. Playing a song front to back ten times logs minutes. Isolating the two beats where the chord change falls apart and drilling only those attacks the weakness.

## Example and what it's good for

A guitarist can't land the G-to-C change in time. Full run-throughs won't fix it, because the change happens once per pass and they're braced for it each time. Instead they loop the transition alone at half speed, watching that the fingers move as a shaped block rather than one at a time. When it lands clean five times running, they nudge the metronome up. The target stays narrow and a little uncomfortable the whole way.

This is how you turn practice from time served into progress made.

## Why it works

Skill improves fastest at the edge of current ability, where attempts sometimes fail and the failures carry information. Easy repetition runs on autopilot and cements whatever you already do, flaws included. Narrowing to one target keeps working memory on the thing that needs to change, and the fast feedback loop lets a student correct before a bad rep sets.

## Problems it solves

- A student practices for hours and plateaus.
  - They're rehearsing strengths and skating past the weak spot. Point them at the one measure that keeps breaking.
- A choir keeps running the whole piece to fix one shaky entrance.
  - Pull that entrance out, drill the breath and the first vowel alone, then fold it back in.

## How to use it

- Diagnose the single limiting weakness before the student practices, so the reps have a target.
- Set a piece hard enough that clean success isn't automatic.
- Build in feedback every few attempts, from a recording, a tuner, or you.
- Slow it down until it's accurate, then rebuild speed. Accurate first, fast second.

## Risks

- It's effortful and rarely fun, so students drift back to playing what they've already mastered. Keep the drills short and the target visible.
- Grinding one weakness with no feedback can carve in a mistake. Confirm each rep is right before piling on repetition.
